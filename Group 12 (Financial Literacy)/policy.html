"Government support & policies"

By: Ronica Robins
March 3, 2023 - 10:34

Government's Impact on the SMMEs sector

The government established the Department of Small Business Development (DSBD) in 2014 to demonstrate its dedication to this industry. The government has committed to a high level of support for small businesses through a combination of tax reductions, subsidies, and free advisory services, notably with the support of the Ministry of Small Business Development.

Tax Matters

Our main goal is to make sure we keep you adequately inform in oder to perform your civic duties, in this page we take you through tax matters. Tax is the primary source of revenue for the South African government and is levied on all income and profits earned by taxpayers, which includes people, businesses, and trusts. Turnover tax, employee taxes (PAYE, UIF, and SDL), and VAT (value-added tax) are the three types of taxes that small businesses must pay.

What qualifies SMMEs in South Africa to pay tax?

According to SARS (South African Revenue Service) the department responisble of tax revenue in the country, a small businesses must register for income tax at the very least if it conducts business as a corporation or close corporation. It must register for PAYE, UIF, and SDL if it employs workers and the total yearly payroll exceeds R 500,000. The small firm must also register for VAT if its annual revenue exceeds R1 million.

What is a Turnover Tax?

To make it simpler for smaller enterprises to comply with their tax duties, the turnover tax system has been streamlined. For small firms with a qualified annual turnover of R 1 million or less, the turnover tax system replaces income tax, VAT, provisional tax, capital gains tax, and dividend tax. However, a microbusiness that has registered for turnover tax has the option to continue using the VAT system

How do small businesses register for turnover tax?

To determine if small enterprises or individuals qualify for turnover tax, they must take a brief test that is available on the SARS website. After determining whether they are eligible for turnover tax, they must complete a TT01 form. The application must be submitted prior to the start of the evaluation year, which spans from March 1 to February 28. If a new microbusiness begins operations during an assessment year and decides it wants to register for turnover tax, it must do so within two months of the business's launch. Existing microbusinesses have until the beginning of the new tax year to register for or switch to turnover tax.

What are the Three Types of Employee Tax?

The main employee taxes in South Africa include PAYE (Pay-As-You-Earn), UIF (Unemployment Insurance Fund), and SDL (Skills Development Levy). Here's a brief explanation of each:

  1. PAYE (Pay-As-You-Earn) - PAYE is the tax deducted by employers from the salaries of their employees on behalf of the South African Revenue Service (SARS). It is a progressive tax system, meaning the rate of deduction increases as an employee's income increases. The deducted amount goes towards income tax, which helps fund government operations and public services.
  2. UIF (Unemployment Insurance Fund) - UIF is a social security program administered by the Department of Employment and Labour in South Africa. It provides short-term financial assistance to workers who become unemployed or are unable to work due to illness, maternity, or adoption leave. The UIF is funded through contributions made by both employees and employers. The contribution rate is set at 2% of an employee's salary, with the employer paying 1% and the employee paying the remaining 1%.
  3. SDL (Skills Development Levy) - SDL is a levy imposed on employers to fund skills development initiatives in South Africa. The levy is 1% of an employer's total payroll and is paid to the relevant Sector Education and Training Authority (SETA) that oversees training and skills development within a specific industry. The SDL aims to improve the quality of the South African workforce by supporting training and education programs.

How do small businesses account for VAT?

On the other hand, VAT (Value-Added Tax) is a consumption tax imposed on the supply of most goods and services in South Africa. It is levied at each stage of production and distribution, but ultimately paid by the end consumer. The current standard VAT rate in South Africa is 15%, although certain goods and services may be exempt or subject to a reduced rate. VAT is collected by registered businesses on behalf of SARS and must be reported and remitted to the tax authorities regularly.

What kinds of documents should small business owners keep for tax purposes?

According to the VAT Act, small enterprises must issue accurate tax invoices, charge their clients VAT at 15%, and pay this amount to SARS on a monthly or bimonthly basis if they are registered for VAT. The input VAT that the small business is charged by its suppliers can be deducted from this payment.

According to SAIT(South African institute Taxation) inaccurate accounting information is one of the the popular tax error among SMMEs. The integrity of a taxpayer's income tax return directly depends on the correctness of the underlying accounting information and supporting records. Due to a lack of accounting function and difficulty separating the financial affairs of the company and the owner, SMMEs often have to deal with tax nightmares which are costly and time consuming.

Small- to medium-sized business owners are responsible for keeping track of all tax-related paperwork, including bank statements, sales invoices, credit notes, supplier invoices, and payroll records. Not only should you preserve these records throughout the tax year for filing purposes, but you also need to keep them for legal reasons. Maintaining your records and information can help you be more accurate when filing your tax returns and will help you plan ahead if you need to pay SARS.

What are the consequences of not complying to regulations?

A penalty that a taxpayer must pay for failing to comply with numerous criteria is known as an administrative non-compliance penalty (Admin Penalty), which carries steep fines and interest on unpaid taxes. An administrative penalty is made up of fixed-amount penalties and percentage-based penalties. For each month that the non-compliance persists, a penalty sum based on a taxpayer's taxable income of between R250 and R16 000 may be assessed.

Currently, the penalty is only levied for late or incomplete returns. This penalty is assessed to every taxpayer who fails to file their return, and they are required to pay it to SARS.
